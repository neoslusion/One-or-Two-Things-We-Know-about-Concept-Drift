\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{ABSTRACT}

Concept drift is a major cause of performance degradation in machine learning systems when data distributions change over time. This thesis studies unsupervised detection, classification, and adaptation for concept drift in streaming data.

The thesis makes two main contributions. First, it improves ShapeDD by using a Standard MMD signal to locate drift candidates and validating those candidates with \textbf{IDW-MMD} (Inverse Density-Weighted MMD) under a Gamma approximation. This design keeps the end-to-end runtime close to the original ShapeDD while giving the validation step a clearer statistical basis. On a 14-dataset detection benchmark with $\delta = 75$ tolerance, the method achieves an F1-score of 0.454.

Second, it proposes \textbf{SE-CDT} (ShapeDD-Enhanced Concept Drift Type), an unsupervised drift type classification method based on the shape of the MMD signal. SE-CDT achieves 82.2\% category accuracy, 65.8\% micro-averaged subcategory accuracy, and 55.7\% macro-averaged subcategory accuracy across five drift types. A concept-memory mechanism improves recurrent-drift identification to 91.5\%, while gradual drift and short blips remain challenging.

Finally, the thesis evaluates a \textbf{Type-Specific Adaptation} framework that selects model-update strategies based on the classified drift type. Compared with no adaptation, it improves accuracy by more than 16 percentage points on both Stepping Drift and Sudden Drift, with statistically significant differences under the Friedman test. The system is also tested as a Kafka-compatible streaming prototype.
