\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{ABSTRACT}

Concept drift is a major cause of performance degradation in machine learning systems when data distributions change over time. This thesis studies unsupervised detection, classification, and adaptation for concept drift in streaming data.

The detection contribution improves ShapeDD by using a Standard MMD signal to locate drift candidates and validating those candidates with \textbf{IDW-MMD} (Inverse Density-Weighted MMD) under a Gamma approximation. This design reduces end-to-end runtime compared with the original ShapeDD while giving the validation step a clearer statistical basis. On a 14-dataset detection benchmark with $\delta = 75$ tolerance and $\text{cooldown} = 150$, the method reaches an F1-score of 0.531.

The classification contribution is \textbf{SE-CDT} (ShapeDD-Enhanced Concept Drift Type), an unsupervised drift-type classifier that reads the shape of the MMD signal. SE-CDT reports 81.5\% category accuracy, 65.6\% micro-averaged and 54.0\% macro-averaged subcategory accuracy across five drift types. A concept-memory mechanism lifts recurrent-drift identification to 89.0\%, while gradual drift and short blips remain the hardest cases.

The adaptation component is a \textbf{Type-Specific Adaptation} framework that selects a model-update strategy based on the classified drift type. Compared with no adaptation, it improves accuracy by more than 16 percentage points on both Stepping Drift and Sudden Drift, with statistically significant differences under the Friedman test. The system is also tested as a Kafka-compatible streaming prototype.
